# Classical Mechanics — Master Question Bank

## Subject Overview

Classical mechanics describes the motion of objects under the influence of forces. It rests on Newton's three laws of motion, which relate forces to changes in velocity. The subject progresses from kinematics (describing motion without worrying about causes) through dynamics (forces and their effects) to energy, momentum, and rotational motion. The framework applies to any everyday-scale, non-relativistic system — from falling objects to orbiting planets. Mastery depends on drawing correct free body diagrams, decomposing vectors, and applying F = ma systematically rather than by intuition.

<details>
<summary>Answers — Newton's Laws</summary>

**Q1.1:** An object continues in its current state of motion (at rest or moving at constant velocity) unless a net external force acts on it. Inertia is an object's resistance to any change in its velocity.

**Q1.2:** F = ma, so a = F/m = 20/5 = 4 m/s².

**Q1.3:** The two forces act on *different objects*. When you push a wall, the wall pushes back on you — one force is on the wall, the other is on you. They act on separate bodies so they cannot cancel. The net force on *you* is the wall's push, which is why you accelerate away.

**Q1.4:** Mass is the measure of how much matter an object contains and is its resistance to acceleration. Unit: kilogram (kg). Weight is the gravitational force acting on that mass: W = mg. Unit: Newton (N). On the Moon, mass is unchanged but weight is about 1/6 of Earth weight because g is smaller.

**Q1.5:** If velocity is constant, acceleration is zero. By F = ma, net force = 0. All forces are balanced — this does not mean there are no forces, only that they sum to zero.

**Q1.6:** Your true weight (gravitational pull) acts downward. The elevator floor exerts a normal force upward. When the elevator accelerates upward, the net upward force must be positive, so the normal force must exceed your gravitational weight. It is that larger normal force from the floor that you feel as heaviness. Using F_net = ma: N - mg = ma, so N = m(g + a).

**Q1.7:** 1. Identify the object and draw a free body diagram showing all forces on it. 2. Set up a coordinate system. 3. Sum all force components along each axis. 4. Apply F_net = ma along each axis separately. 5. Solve for the unknown. 6. Check units and sign conventions.

</details>
